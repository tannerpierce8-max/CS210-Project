DriverPass System Analysis & Design

Project Summary

The DriverPass project was completed for a client named DriverPass, a company focused on helping students pass their driving exams. The client wanted a system that would provide online practice tests and allow students to schedule on-the-road training sessions. The goal was to reduce the high failure rate of driving test applicants by creating a more structured and interactive preparation system.

The system needed to support multiple stakeholders, including students, instructors, management, and IT administrators. It required secure user account management, scheduling functionality, reporting features, and integration with DMV practice exam content.

What I Did Well

One area I performed particularly well in was translating the client interview into structured, organized documentation. I clearly identified functional and nonfunctional requirements and ensured they aligned directly with the client’s concerns.

In the system design document, I effectively used UML diagrams and system modeling to represent processes, users, and system interactions. I made sure each design component traced back to a documented requirement, demonstrating strong systems thinking and alignment between analysis and design.

What I Would Revise

If I were to revise one part of my work, I would enhance the level of detail in the nonfunctional requirements section. While I addressed performance, security, and usability, I would expand those areas further with measurable metrics such as response time thresholds or uptime percentages.

Adding quantifiable benchmarks would strengthen the professional quality of the document and make it more implementation-ready.

Interpreting User Needs

I interpreted the user’s needs by carefully analyzing the interview transcript and identifying pain points, business goals, and operational constraints. From there, I translated those needs into clear functional requirements such as user authentication, scheduling capabilities, reporting dashboards, and administrative controls.

Considering user needs is critical because a technically sound system that does not solve the user’s actual problem will ultimately fail. Systems must be built around real-world workflows and stakeholder expectations.

My Approach to Designing Software

When approaching system design, I begin by gathering and organizing requirements before thinking about implementation. I focus on understanding stakeholders, identifying system boundaries, and defining inputs, outputs, and interactions.

I use structured techniques such as requirements analysis, use case modeling, UML diagrams (use case, sequence, and class diagrams), and iterative refinement of system components. In future projects, I will continue applying Agile principles to validate requirements early and refine designs through stakeholder feedback.
